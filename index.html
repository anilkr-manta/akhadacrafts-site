The craft

Teak wood — chosen for a reason

Teak (Tectona grandis) has been the timber of choice for demanding tools and structures for over two thousand years. Its natural silica content and dense grain make it naturally resistant to moisture, cracking, and the stress of repeated impact — ideal for a swinging tool that absorbs force with every rep.

Each piece is shaped and balanced by hand using traditional methods. No CNC. No composite materials. The weight distribution and taper geometry follow the proportions used in traditional Indian akharas.

What each pair is good for
1.1 lb / 500 g — Wrist, joint & rehab
The entry point. Used for pendulum swings, wrist circles, and light arm casts. Anyone coming back from a shoulder, elbow, or wrist injury should start here and stay for several weeks. Also excellent for desk workers to counteract postural tightening.
1.8 lb / 800 g — Shoulder mobility
The classic weight for traditional British military patterns — mills, figure-eights, and extended arm circles. Builds full rotator cuff stability and restores complete shoulder range of motion. Best for daily warm-up routines.
3.3 lb / 1.5 kg — Strength endurance
Where training becomes genuinely demanding. The extended lever arm and 3.3 lb weight create far more shoulder load than the same weight in a dumbbell. Use for flows, sport-specific rotational work, and high-rep swinging patterns.
4.4 lb / 2 kg — Full conditioning
The top of the beginner kit range. Grip, forearm, shoulder, and core all engaged at once. The Royal Marines PTI badge features clubs at this weight. Use for low-rep strength sets and finishers after heavier barbell or grappling work.

Why train with these tools

What modern training misses

Indian clubs, mugdar, and samtola address movement patterns and joint angles that barbells, dumbbells, and machines simply cannot reach.

01
Full shoulder range of motion
Most pressing and pulling exercises work the shoulder through a narrow arc. Club swinging uses the full 360° range of the shoulder joint — the way it was designed to move — building strength and flexibility at the same time.
02
Rotator cuff health & prehab
The stabiliser muscles of the rotator cuff are chronically undertrained in gym environments. Light club work (500 g–800 g) directly targets these muscles with low impact, making it one of the most effective shoulder injury prevention tools available.
03
Grip & forearm strength
Holding a weight at the end of a handle rather than in the centre of your fist recruits the forearm and hand differently. Forearms will fatigue faster than with an equivalent dumbbell — which is precisely the point.
04
Core anti-rotation stability
Swinging an off-centre load forces the core to resist rotation constantly. This trains the stabilising function of the abdominals — the function that actually protects the spine — rather than just the flexion-focused crunching motion.
05
Coordination & body awareness
Learning complex swing patterns requires genuine neurological effort. The coordination challenge keeps the brain engaged in the same way martial arts or juggling does — and the payoff carries over to sport performance and everyday movement quality.
06
How to fit it into training
Use light clubs (500 g–800 g) as a 10-minute warm-up before any upper body session. Use heavier clubs and mugdar as standalone conditioning sessions or as finishers. The samtola works best as a balance and stability drill on active rest days.
