The material

Why teak. Not pine. Not rubber.

Teak (Tectona grandis) has been the timber of choice for demanding work for over two thousand years — ship decks, royal furniture, weapons. Its natural silica content and interlocked grain resist cracking and splitting under the repeated impact stress of heavy swinging tools.

Teak also produces its own natural oils, making it resistant to moisture without any lacquer or coating. Your mugdar won't warp in a humid gym. Your clubs won't dry and crack in an air-conditioned room. The wood maintains itself.

Every piece is shaped on a traditional lathe, not CNC-machined. The taper geometry, the handle diameter, the weight distribution — calibrated by hand and eye, following proportions refined in Indian strength training over generations.

What each pair is best for
1.1 lb / 500 g — Rehab & coordination
The safest starting point. Pendulum swings, wrist circles, light arm casts. Ideal for anyone returning from shoulder, elbow, or wrist injury — and for desk workers whose joints need daily movement. Use for 10–20 minutes every day with no fatigue.
1.8 lb / 800 g — Shoulder mobility
The classic weight for traditional military club patterns — mills, figure-eights, arm casts, and full-circle swings. Builds rotator cuff stability and restores shoulder range of motion lost from pressing-heavy gym training. Best as a daily warm-up ritual.
3.3 lb / 1.5 kg — Strength endurance
Where training becomes genuinely demanding. The long lever multiplies the felt weight — 3.3 lb here is harder than 3.3 lb in a dumbbell. Use for flowing rotational patterns, sport-specific shoulder work, and conditioning drills.
4.4 lb / 2 kg — Full conditioning
The top of the beginner range. Grip, forearm, shoulder, and core all load simultaneously. The Royal Marines PTI branch trains with clubs at this weight. Use for low-rep strength sets and as a finisher after heavier barbell or grappling sessions.

Why train with these

What modern training doesn't give you

These tools target movement patterns, joint angles, and stabiliser muscles that barbells, dumbbells, and machines cannot reach.

01
Full 360° shoulder range
Most gym movements work the shoulder through a narrow arc. Club swinging uses the full range the shoulder was designed for — building strength and flexibility simultaneously, not trading one for the other.
02
Rotator cuff health & prehab
The stabiliser muscles of the rotator cuff are chronically undertrained in conventional gym programmes. Light clubs (500–800 g) target these muscles directly with low impact — one of the most effective shoulder injury prevention tools available.
03
Grip & forearm strength
Holding weight at the end of a long handle versus in the centre of your palm loads the forearm completely differently. Forearms will fatigue faster than with an equivalent dumbbell — which is exactly the training effect these tools were designed for.
04
Core anti-rotation stability
Swinging an off-centre load forces the core to resist rotation continuously. This trains the stabilising function of the abdominals — the function that actually protects the spine — not just the flexion-focused crunch motion most programmes default to.
05
Coordination & neural training
Mastering complex swing patterns demands genuine neurological effort. The challenge keeps the brain engaged the way martial arts training does — and coordination gains carry over to sport performance, reaction time, and movement quality in everyday life.
06
How to fit it in
Use light clubs (500–800 g) as a 10-minute daily warm-up before any upper body session. Use heavier clubs and mugdar as standalone conditioning or finishers. The samtola works best as a proprioception drill on active rest days alongside bodyweight work.
